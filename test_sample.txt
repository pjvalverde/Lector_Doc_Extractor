The most powerful force in investing is compound interest. Albert Einstein reportedly 
called it the eighth wonder of the world. Warren Buffett built his fortune not through 
brilliant stock picks alone, but through decades of patient compounding.

The key insight is that wealth building is more about time in the market than timing 
the market. A dollar invested at age 20 is worth far more than a dollar invested at 40, 
even if the later investment earns a higher return. This is because compounding is 
exponential, not linear.

Consider this example: if you invest $10,000 at a 10% annual return, after 30 years 
you'll have approximately $174,000. But if you wait just 10 years to start, you'll 
only have about $67,000 — less than half.

The central lesson is clear: start early, be consistent, and let time do the heavy 
lifting. Don't try to get rich quick. The tortoise beats the hare in investing, just 
as in the fable. Patience is not just a virtue in investing — it is THE virtue.
